Wine Tasting Experience

The below wine tasting experience offers the opportunity for wine enthusiasts at any expertise level to enjoy and learn more about wine.

The ritual of peering into a glass and swirling it around and then sniffing it warily, before taking a sip - only to spit it out. It looks, well, highly suspicious (if not silly). However, this sequence of perfectly logical steps can immeasurably enhance the enjoyment of a good, or great, wine (think of it like test driving the car of your dreams before the purchase). To begin, we don’t pour a full glass for tasting (about a third or less is about right), we need room for swirling. Then we take a good look at the wine by holding the glass toward the light. See if it is clear or cloudy ? Does it contain sediment or other solid matter ? Is it clear or brownish at the rim ? (again, it’s like walking around the new car you are about to purchase). Next, is swirling and sniffing. Swirl it gently to release the bouquet, then place your nose inside the glass and sniff it as you would a flower. Try to identify the scents and flavors that are released. Be keen to identify any musty, moldy or vinegary smells (Think of it as enjoying the “new car” smell). Now to the tasting. Take a small sip and swirl it around in your closed mouth. Roll it over all the taste sensitive parts of your tongue. The sweet sensors are on the tip; in the middle you will register saltiness with acidity and sourness on the sides. Any bitterness is registered in the back. Then combine the bouquet your nose discovered with the taste sensations provided by your tongue. Be aware of your thoughts from these sensations. In fact, some folks write down these thoughts and sensations in a tasting journal so they can refer back to them for future tastings or pairings. Your goal is to try and identify any of the five principle elements gathered from the tasting experience: Dryness/sweetness, Acidity, Tannins, Oak and Fruit. Okay, let’s POUR !
